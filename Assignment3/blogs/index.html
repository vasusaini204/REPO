HISTORY OF SANGANERI

It is believed that the artisans, who belong to the Chhippa community, draw lineage from the artisans of Kathiawar in Gujarat, and historical records also support their Gujarat connection. The social upheavals that occurred during the wars of the Mughals and the Marathas in the mid 17th century, followed by incursions of ideas between Gujarat and Rajasthan fostered aesthetic similarities in their craft.


Selected for its abundance of soft water and clay suitable for the process of sun bleaching fabrics, Sanganer was developed into a major printing centre under the patronage of the Jaipur royal family. It is possible that Sawai Jai Singh (1700-44), who founded the city of Jaipur in 1727 A.D. and promoted various crafts in his kingdom, contributed to the growth of textile printing in Sanganer during the early 18th century. (Jasminder Kaur, 2017)

MOTIFS

Sanganeri hand block prints are renowned for their finesse in small, decorative and delicate floral patterns printed on fine cotton, known as Buti and Buta, Bel and Jaal.




These patterns consist of intricate motifs inspired by a variety of flora and fauna. Supplied with a generous amount of influences from the Mughal era, English and Dutch East India Companies and European culture, an extensive and unique motif vocabulary has developed in Sanganeri prints.


PIGMENTS

While presently, synthetic and chemical dyes are mainly used for Sanganeri printing to meet the growing demand for unique colours from consumers, the traditional Sanganeri printing used natural dyes. These dyes were derived from natural sources like yellow from turmeric, blue from the indigo plant, black from rust iron, and so on. Red and black were the most common colours used throughout the state of Rajasthan, followed by yellow, blue and green. Apart from being eco-friendly, these colours had immense medicinal value.
